Project № 02 Professional 2022 — present

P&V — Insurance Data Work

Four years inside a Belgian mutual insurer — first as a data steward resolving the cases AI couldn't decide, now as an analyst working with the data that emerges. Specifics anonymized; the methodology and what I took from each piece described in detail.

Note: the case descriptions below describe the shape of the work. Specific figures are placeholders until they can be replaced with figures cleared for external mention. The broader methodology is accurate.

2022 — 2024 · Data Steward

Working on the cases AI handed back.

At P&V, automated systems handle the data work that fits a pattern. What landed on my desk for two years was the opposite: records that wouldn't merge across systems, fields that disagreed, customer entities that the model couldn't reconcile with confidence. The "uncertain" pile.

The work was deliberate and slow. Each case required understanding the upstream systems that had produced the conflict — different definitions of "customer," different date conventions, different identifier schemes left over from legacy migrations. Resolution was rarely a database operation. It was a judgement call about which version of the data better represented what had actually happened in the world.

What I took from it: the cases AI hands back are the most interesting cases in the dataset. They're where definitions are weak, where systems disagree, where the business has changed faster than the data model. Resolving them well requires a kind of judgement that doesn't show up in a confidence score. Doing this work for two years permanently shifted how I read any analysis: I now ask first what was excluded as "uncertain" before reading the confident middle.

2024 — present · Data Analyst

Turning resolved data into business decisions.

Now I work as an analyst on the same data I once stewarded. The transition surfaced something useful: most of the analytical work that used to feel impressive is downstream of the steward work. If the input is clean, the analysis becomes much more about choosing the right question than executing the technique.

Recurring work includes pattern analysis on claims data, customer behaviour segmentation across product lines, and ad-hoc analyses supporting business decisions. SQL is the primary tool; Python with pandas for non-trivial transformations and one-off investigations; BI tooling for delivery to stakeholders who need to interact with the results themselves.

What I take from it: The most useful analyses aren't the ones with the most sophisticated technique. They're the ones that change what someone does on Monday morning. Doing this in insurance specifically keeps the stakes real — the decisions downstream of the analysis are priced in actual currency, paid to or refused from actual customers.

Reflection on four years

Two observations that I think generalise beyond P&V:

  • The most underrated career move in data is starting as a steward. You learn why the numbers are wrong before you learn how to use them. Most analysts inherit clean-looking data and never develop the muscle to question it.
  • Once you adapt to any role, the work starts to take on the same shape. Different domain, similar pattern. This is why I'm now actively looking for work that's different on purpose — closer to building tools than producing reports.

"AI handles the patterns. What stays on a human desk is the texture — the cases that don't fit. Most real decisions live in that pile."
