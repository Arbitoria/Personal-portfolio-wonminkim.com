ARBITORIA

A personal-finance transparency platform across six countries. Salary, housing, utilities, insurance, loans, investing — built on official data, deliberately review-free, available in seventeen language combinations. Maître de vos propres choix — l'art de la vie choisie.

Problem

Most financial decisions ordinary people make happen under information asymmetry that doesn't need to exist. The data already exists — government statistics on salaries, public regulator filings on insurance prices, official utility tariffs, central bank rate disclosures. What's missing is the translation layer that turns it into something a person can actually compare against their own situation.

Existing tools fail in two predictable ways. First, they operate inside a single country, which is useless for the growing population of people who live across borders. Second, they're contaminated with reviews — opinions priced as data, anecdotes weighted as evidence. Reviews look helpful but introduce noise, and noise is the opposite of what someone making a real financial decision needs.

Approach

ARBITORIA is built on three commitments:

  • Official data only. Every figure on the platform traces back to a public, authoritative source — national statistics offices, central banks, regulator filings, government tax tables.
  • No reviews, ever. Reviews introduce noise that compromises transparency. The platform shows what exists, not what someone felt about it. People can form their own judgement once they can see the actual data.
  • Cross-country by design. Six countries (Korea, Japan, U.S., France, Spain, Belgium) with seventeen language combinations. The same person can compare their situation in their language across the markets they're connected to.

What's on the platform

The product is organised around six categories — the decisions that consume most of a household's financial attention:

  • Salary — gross-to-net calculators by country, top jobs by market, cross-country comparisons
  • Housing — rent vs. purchase trade-offs, mortgage calculators with timeline-based interest projections
  • Utilities — electricity, water, internet, mobile — provider-by-provider comparisons
  • Insurance — coverage and price comparisons
  • Loans — interest calculators showing real cost over time
  • Investing — official-data-driven recommendations on funds and savings vehicles

Tech stack

Frontend: React (Vite) · multi-locale
Backend: FastAPI (Python)
Database: PostgreSQL
Data sources: National statistics offices, regulator filings, central banks
Deploy: Vercel

What I learned

The hardest part of cross-country financial transparency isn't the data ingestion — it's the comparability. Two countries' "average salary" figures rarely use the same definition. Tax structures differ in ways that aren't captured by gross-to-net alone. Even something as basic as "rent per month" is reported differently across markets.

Building ARBITORIA was the hands-on version of the lesson my data-steward years gave me: data quality isn't a one-time fix. It's the ongoing translation between what sources publish and what users actually need to compare. The work that AI can't finish is the same work, in a different domain.

"Reviews look helpful but introduce noise. People making real financial decisions don't need more opinions — they need to see what exists."

Status

Live at arbitoria.com. Six countries supported, seventeen language combinations. Active development — new categories added as the underlying data sources are validated and integrated.

Why it matters to me

ARBITORIA is the smallest version of what I want to do professionally. Most of working with data is descriptive — explaining what already happened, predicting what's likely to happen next. Building ARBITORIA is the opposite: making a thing that didn't exist, that materially changes what someone can decide. That's the side of the verb I want my next role to be on.
